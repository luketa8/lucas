Safelink

Transforming accessibility and engagement for equal opportunity. Website and app redesign for the free phone social program.

The Lifeline free phone program is a government assistance program in the United States that provides eligible low-income, disabled individuals, and veterans with a free or discounted cell phone and monthly service to ensure they have access to essential communication services.

Period

2014-2016

Role

ux/ui design, product design, front-end development

Tools:

Sketch, Adobe Illustrator.

Time for redesign

My first proposal after joining the Safelink redesign team was to start the signup process by asking for the Zip code on the home page. This streamlined the verification process as it allowed for the application to quickly identify the applicant's eligibility requirements, based on their geographic location before proceeding with the rest of the application.

The “Free Phone Program” title and the human element created an inviting tone to engage the user. By reducing the number of steps and removing unnecessary form fields, the new UX/UI design significantly improved the user experience.

Enhanced Accessibility

The majority of users were not accustomed to navigating user interfaces and using technology. And a large number of them are visually impaired or else.

So accessibility was a key focus of the UX/UI redesign. The new design adhered to WCAG guidelines, ensuring that individuals with disabilities can easily navigate and access the Safelink.com platform. Features such as adjustable font sizes, high color contrast, and keyboard navigation options were implemented, enabling a more inclusive experience for all users.

I proposed implementing a third-party tool for web accessibility after doing research and then worked on the implementation.

KPI Detail

I redesigned the key performance indicator user flow, adding a search field and a moved the scenario creation tool to separate screen in order to keep the UI clean and consistent.

The new design combined different targets, scenarios, user types and categories in an intuitive layout. This empowered users to adapt the app to their operational requirements and focus on the metrics that matter most to them.

When everything is an alarm

I split the alarms experience into three focused areas: an alarm center, an alarm definition tool, and an alarm history view. Previously, all actions lived on a single screen, which made critical tasks slow and increased the risk of missed or misinterpreted alerts. Separating these flows reduced immediacy, but improved clarity in situations where errors have real operational consequences.

I added contextual details to each alarm, such as the related KPI and breach threshold, so users could assess severity at a glance. Actionable notifications supported immediate response, including escalation paths and corrective actions. Visual indicators helped users spot trends, compare sites or time periods, and identify patterns behind recurring issues.

Designing the UI, building the product

Building on the existing brand, I expanded its visual language into a production-ready user interface. I designed color systems for alarms, dashboards, and data types to clearly communicate status and priority, particularly in high-risk scenarios. Reusable UI components were created to ensure consistency across the app and support faster, more reliable development.

In a small, fast-moving team, this meant taking ownership beyond design and translating decisions directly into code. The application was built in React, and I implemented the visual layer using Material UI to maintain fidelity between design intent and the shipped product.
